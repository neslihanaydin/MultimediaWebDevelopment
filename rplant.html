Monstera, Swiss cheese plant

Be ready to create a lot of space even if you just have a small amount of monstera because it is a powerful jungle climber. Your monstera will eventually grow from a small sprout with glossy leaves to a height of at least 6 feet. The monstera plant has heart-shaped leaves that can be up to 12 inches broad. As the foliage ages, it develops dramatic windows and deeply cut lobes that give it titles like "Swiss cheese plant" and "split-leaf philodendron." A dramatic monstera will provide drama and a big personality to any room that is bright and open.

HELP ME GROW

Your small monstera will remain growing, green, and lovely under bright, filtered, or indirect light. Although its large leaves can't handle the full summer heat, they will benefit from a few hours of direct sunlight in the winter. While tolerating moderate illumination, this climber. It will produce fewer, smaller, and less-holey leaves on elongated, spindly stalks to show that it wants more sun. When the air around your monstera is warmer than 70 to 75°F, it will benefit from additional humidity. Your monstera will be at ease in an environment that is normal to warm (65 to 80°F). To keep its leaves from drying out in the dry indoor air, give it a tray of wet pebbles and a light spritz every so often.

FEED ME

During the spring and summer, let the top inch of your monstera's potting soil dry out between sips. In the winter, when its development will be slower, minimise watering a little, but keep an eye out for signs that it is too dry or suffering from low humidity. From early spring until early fall, give your monstera a balanced fertiliser every two or three weeks. Fertilize once a month in the winter.

LOVE ME

To replicate the trees monstera clambers on in its natural habitat, provide a strong climbing post coated in moss. As your monstera spreads upward, gently attach stems to the post and direct aerial roots into the moss. Please use Polish: To keep them clean and at their best, regularly wipe the glossy leaves of your monstera with a moist cloth.

Aloe vera

It is commonly known that the gel-like substance found inside the succulent leaves of aloe plants has soothing effects on the skin. Aloe is still sought after by those with sunburns who need soothing relief because Cleopatra supposedly employed it in her beauty regimen. If healing that painful burn isn't enough, aloe has another clever trick to show off: it will reward your tender attention with cuddly grand-aloes, or pups.

HELP ME GROW

Your aloe will benefit from bright light, such as that coming from a south-facing window. Aloe is adaptable to most common household temperatures and isn't finicky about humidity. Wintertime cooler temperatures, less watering, and a break in feedings provide it a healthy nap time. For keeping aloe comfortable in its pot, use a well-drained, sandy to gritty soil-based potting mix. The risk of rotting is decreased by adding a layer of pebbles or coarse sand on top of the soil to assist moisture drain out fast from the base of the leaves.

FEED ME

In between waterings, let the top inch of the soil in the plant's container dry out. Aloe prefers even moisture, but a wet environment makes it unhappy and susceptible to rot. Reduce irrigation during the winter. From spring until the beginning of fall, fertilise aloe monthly with houseplant fertiliser. During the cooler months, when its development will slow down, give your aloe a rest from fertilisation. Resuming in the spring when the light is more abundant.

LOVE ME

If your aloe is happy, it will generate pups, also known as baby plants or offsets, which grow from the main plant's base. Separate the pups from the ecstatic parent aloe using a sharp knife, and then let the wounded surfaces heal for a day or two before planting them in individual pots.
The key is consistency: If you take care of aloe, which is long-lasting and easy to cultivate, it will be your chlorophyll friend for a very long time. Aloe thrives in bright light, but oddly, if you transfer it suddenly from indoors to outside, the sun might burn it. Aloe can become sad and pale when moved to a location with less light.

Palm

In the Victorian times, palms gained popularity as indoor plants, but these ancient, primarily tropical plants are still attractive complements to any home with enough room and care.

However, if your bouncing young sprout is a palm with vertical growth potential, you'll want to make sure you have plenty of area. A palm species that grows enormous in an outdoor setting may remain at more manageable heights when limited by a pot. Because of the manner that palms develop, trimming them back when they get too tall won't suffice because it may harm or even kill the plant.

HELP ME GROW

Most palms will grow in bright light, including a few hours of direct sunlight each day, but each species has different lighting requirements. A sudden change in the light can send your palm into a dark, highly dangerous depression. The palm that you bring home is probably a young plant that would typically be covered by higher vegetation. It has also become used to life and light indoors, so it won't be happy to discover itself in direct sunlight on a balcony. A palm that has been growing outside may also suffer if it is suddenly relocated into a room with poor illumination. Keep palms that spend the summer outdoors in a shady area and gradually adjust their posture.

FEED ME

If you feed your palm drinks of distilled, filtered, or rainfall, it will be happiest. Palms might be a little picky about their water. Most palms have modest feeding needs that are best met with a spring serving of slow-release fertiliser and occasionally adding compost to the surface of their potting mix. Watering needs vary by species, but most enjoy lightly moist soil during the spring and summer months and reduced watering in fall and winter, when growth typically slows. Too much fertiliser might harm the roots of your palm by causing salts to build up in the potting soil. It is useful to flush away extra salts from the soil a few times per year by flooding it with water, letting it drain, and then repeating the process.

LOVE ME

The Kentia palm is a gorgeous upright palm with glossy, dark green leaves that can reach heights of 8 feet or more. However, due to its slow growth, it may take many years before its fronds brush your ceiling. The Kentia palm can tolerate low lighting but prefers bright, indirect light. Your kentia palm will increase extremely slowly in low light exposures. If your kentia displays pale foliage and laziness, it may be too dark; add more light or place it next to a window with good light. Kentia palms dislike having their rather delicate roots disturbed and want to fit tightly in the pot.

Dracaenas

Diverse dracaenas produce effects ranging from fanciful to formal with their colorfully stripy ribbonlike leaves arching from stems. This group of foliage plants includes forms to fit almost any decor and care requirements even novice plant parents can handle. Most dracaenas produce their pointed leaves in a spiral pattern around a woody stem that culminates in a rosette of foliage. As plants age, they tend to lose their lower leaves, exposing a stem decorated bu triangular leaf scars; the resulting effect is palmlike, but easier to care for.

HELP ME GROW

Indirect bright to moderate lighting will help your dracaena maintain its pretty colors, and it will be happiest fit receives a couple hours of filtered sunlight daily. While your dracaena can survive in low light, it won't grow up to make you proud without adequate lighting. Pale leaves and fading stripes are signs that conditions are too dim.

FEED ME

Water regularly to keep the soil evenly moist, but steer clear of overwatering and soggy conditions. Nourish with a balanced fertilizer every 2-3 weeks from spring through summer. Reduce feedings to once a month in fall and winter.

LOVE ME

The handsome corn plant (Dracaena fragrans 'Massangeana') has arching, broad, pointed leaves with lengthwise yellow stripes that bear a resemblance to the leaves of corn in the field. This effect is heightened by the dracaena's sturdy, upright habit; mature plants may reach 6 feet tall. The narrow leaves of Madagascar dragon tree (Dracaena marginata) are dark green with thin red-purple edges; the variety 'Tricolor' has narrow stripes of pink and cream running the length of its leaves. A slender, easygoing dracaena, your little dragon tree can grow up to 5 feet tall to deliver an attractive vertical accent in your home with woody stems topped by a pleasingly quirky mop of spiky colorful leaves. Although their grassy foliage may attract nibbles from your fur-family members, dracaenas contain substances that are toxic to dogs and cats and should be placed out of pets' reach.

Peace lilies

Refined good looks and modest care requirements have earned spathiphylums, commonly known as peace lilies or spaths, a place in malls, offices, and other commercial settings around the globe. For plant parents in need of a confidence boost or those who are looking for a sprout that can make the best of a low: light location, a peace lily will respond to your loving care with a fountain of glossy green leaves and long-lasting white tear-drop-shaped spathes, each clasped around a creamy yellow flower stalk. Varieties range from towering 6-footers to petite selections that may grow no more than 12 inches tall.

HELP ME GROW

Adaptability to a range of lighting conditions has contributed to peace lily's popularity; it will get along under artificial lights and in moderate natural lighting, as from a north-facing window. Your own pretty peace lily will be happier, though, if it receives moderate to bright, indirect light, at least in spring and summer when it is actively growing. Peace lily may develop brown, dried patches on its foliage in response to too much direct sun.

FEED ME

Water your peace lily when the top half inch of its potting soil has dried out. Evenly, lightly moist soil throughout spring and summer will keep your spath happy. In winter, water less frequently, but don't let the soil dry out completely. Your peace lily will signal extreme thirst by drooping dramatically and will recover equally dramatically when you supply it with water. But repeated wilting is tough on any plant.

LOVE ME

Give your baby a warm shower once in a while to freshen its foliage and, in between showers, gently wipe leaves clean with a damp cloth. More, please: A peace lily that wilts often in spite of appropriate watering may be telling you its roots are too crowded. Divide very pot-bound plants in spring, carefully separating clumps of leaves attached to shallow rhizomes. Replant at the same depth in fresh potting soil. Let divisions settle in for 2-3 months before fertilizing.
